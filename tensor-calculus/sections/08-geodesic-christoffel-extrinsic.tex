\section{Geodesics and Christoffel Symbols in Extrinsic Geometry}

In these notes we'll cover Geodesics and Christoffel Symbols in extrinsic geometry, so we're going 
to consider 2D surfaces living in a 3D space, and we'll cover the same using intrinsic geometry later.\\

This is also the first step to understanding the concept of Covariant Derivative, but we're getting ahead of ourselves...\\

\textbf{Let's start by defining what a geodesic is, a geodesic is the answer to the question of finding the shortest distance
between two points.}\\

In a flat space, this is pretty easy, the shortest distance between two points is a straight line connecting the two, in mathematical formulation, we 
can write, considering two points defined by vectors $\color{teal}\vec{a}$ and $\color{violet}\vec{b}$:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{a}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup(\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{b}\endgroup - \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{a}\endgroup)
\end{equation}

Different values of $\color{brown}\lambda$ gets us any point on the same line.\\

But we generally cannot draw straight lines on a curved 2D surface, so the process of finding the shortest distance 
between two points involves finding a special curve called the \textbf{geodesic}, which is basically the straightest possible path
in a curved surface/space, that also minimizes the distance between two points.\\

To start our discussion on geodesics, we can ask ourselves a pretty basic question: how do we know if a path is straight in a flat space? To answer this, we're 
going to take a look at both velocity and acceleration vectors.

An image talks more than words:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/flat-hills.png}
\end{center}

In flat space, a straight path has \textbf{zero acceleration} when we travel along it at constant speed.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/curved-hills.png}
\end{center}

In curved space, a straight path has \textbf{zero tangential acceleration} when we travel along it at constant speed.
The acceleration vector is always pointing out of the surface in the normal direction, for a geodesic curve.\\

And now to the computation of them, we basically need to find curves where the acceleration vector is normal to the surface, so if 
we consider the extrinsic approach, that would mean that, represending the acceleration vector as composed by 
two parts, tangential and normal components, the tangential one would need to be zero:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} = 
    \cancel{\left(\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\right)^{tangential}} +
    \left(\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\right)^{normal}
\end{align*}

Recall that we've seen how to define a 2D surface using the coordinate system uv and 
the derivatives of the position vector with respect to these u and v coordinates are the tangent basis vectors $\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup$.\\

So to look at the acceleration vector, we need to compute the second order derivative of the position vector with respect to the parameter $\lambda$. We already computed the velocity vector in the 
tangent plane, in \ref{eq:velocity-vector}, using the multi-variable chain-rule:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} 
    = \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
\end{equation}

So to get the acceleration vector, we compute the derivative:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right) &=
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)
\end{align*}

The terms containing the second order derivatives of $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$ are okay to understand, but the other two terms, where we see the derivative
of the basis vectors with respect to the parameter $\color{brown}\lambda$ require some additional work to figure out correctly.\\
We know from multi-variable chain rule, that the $\color{brown}\lambda$ derivative operator, can be explanded out as a linear combination of 
$\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$ derivative operators:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}
\end{equation}

So, the two derivatives above can be expressed as:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} 
    &= \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\\
    &= \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup^2}
    + \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\\\\
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} 
    &= \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\\
    &= \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}
    + \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup^2}
\end{align*}

So we can now substitute in the previous expression of the acceleration vector:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right) &= 
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup^2}
    + \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}
    + \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup^2}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} 
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup^2}
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}
    + \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}
\end{align*}

To get a bit more compact notation, we can define $\color{red}u^1 = u$ and $\begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup = \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup$ and using Einstein summation convention:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup^2} &=
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}
\end{align*}

Now, the first terms (only summed over index i) are just coefficients (second order derivatives) multiplied by the tangent vectors $\frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}$, so are
a linear combination of the tangent vectors, hence we're sure they're purely lying on the tangent plane to the surface.\\
The same is not valid for the other terms (summed both over i and j), as they measure how the tangent vectors themselves change, so it can have both a tangent component, but also a normal component to the tangent plane on the surface.\\

Let's then study these terms, and we're going to do to by expaning these in some basis. At the end of the day these are 3D vectors and can be expanded, for example, on the X, Y, Z coordinates we've seen, but we prefer
to expand these in the tangent plane using the tangent plane basis vectors themselves $\frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}$.

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/tangent-plane.png}
\end{center}

So we can write these as linear combination with some coefficients we define as $\Gamma$ for the tangent components, and $L$ for the normal one.

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} &=
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} 
    + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}
    + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
\end{align*}

The $\Gamma$ coefficients are called \textbf{Christoffel symbols} and gives us the tangential components of that second order derivative, whereas the $L$ coefficients are the coefficients of the \textbf{second fundamental form}, 
which gives us the normal components of that.\\

We can derive the Christoffel symbols by considering that the normal vector is by definition normal to the tangent basis vectors, so it means that their dot product is 0, we can use this fact
and perform the dot product on both sides:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup} &= 
    \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\right) \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup}\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup}\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup
\end{align*}

To isolate the Christoffel symbols, we need to get rid of the metric tensor on the right side, and we can do so by recalling the defintion 
of the inverse metric tensor, so:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{lm}\endgroup &= 
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup\begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{lm}\endgroup\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \delta^{m}_k\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{m}_{ij}\endgroup
\end{align*}

So we have:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{m}_{ij}\endgroup = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{lm}\endgroup
\end{equation}

In a similar way we can derive the coefficients of the second fundamental forms, this time with the dot product with the normal vector itself:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup &= 
    \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\right) \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup
\end{align*}

So we have, also considering a more concrete expression for the normal vector to the tangent plane of the surface:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cross \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\|\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cross \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup \|}
\end{equation}

We can finally plug these additional terms into the equation of the second order derivative of the position vector, to end up with:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup^2} &=
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\\
    &= \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} 
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\right)
\end{align*}

If you notice, in this equation, all the terms are either given using the tangent vectors, or the normal vector, so we can group the terms together:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup^2} &=
    \left(\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right) \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}
    + \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Our main objective was to find geodesic curves, which are curves where the acceleration vector is only normal to the surface. What this means is that the tangential term of the above
equation, in order to satisfy the geodesic requirement, has to be 0:

\begin{equation}\label{eq:geodesic}
    \boxed{\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 }
\end{equation}

\subsection{Geodesic Examples on Plane and Sphere}

This section gives examples on the actual computation of geodesics, and the plan to do so is:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Compute Christoffel symbols
    \begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^l\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{lk}\endgroup }
    \end{equation}
    which gives us information on how the basis vectors change from point to point on the surface.
    \item Solve Geodesic equations
    \begin{equation}
    \boxed{\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 }
    \end{equation}
    Plugging the Christoffel symbols and solve for the path, will is a set of u-coordinates in function of the path parameter $\lambda$  
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Geodesics on a plane}
Let's start with computing geodesics on a plane surface. This is more a sanity check to actually verify that the geodesic equation is a simple 
straight line lying on the plane itself, as we already know.\\

The equation of a 2D plane is given by:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup (\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) = \vec{p} + \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup
\end{equation}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/2d-plane.png}
\end{center}

To compute the Christoffel symbols, we need to compute the first and second order derivative of the position vector
with respect to the plane's coordinates $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$, which are easily calculated as:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} = \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup, \quad \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} = \begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup\\
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup^2} = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup^2} = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} = \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} = \vec{0}
\end{align*}

Since the Christoffel symbols depend on those second order derivative, and they're all zero, that means that on the 2D plane:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup = 0
\end{equation}

If you think about it, it actually makes sense since Christoffel symbols track how the basis vectors change from point to point, and on a 2D surface, 
the basis vectors are just constant in every point in space, hence Christoffel symbols are correctly zero.\\

This makes it easier to compute the geodesic equations, as this only depends on the second-order derivative of the coordinates wrt the parameter:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \cancel{\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}} = 0 
\end{align*}

In other words, geodesics on the plane, are curve for which acceleration is zero.\\

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} &= 0\\
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} &= 0
\end{align*}

The solution to these differential equations would just be linear equations for both $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}k_u\endgroup\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}u_0\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup = \begingroup\color{teal}k_v\endgroup\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}v_0\endgroup
\end{align*}

where the numbers $\color{red}k_u$, $\color{red}u_0$, $\color{teal}k_v$, $\color{teal}v_0$ are just constants.\\
Plugging these into the 2D plane equation:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup (\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) &= \vec{p} + \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup\\
    &= \vec{p} + \left(\begingroup\color{red}k_u\endgroup\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}u_0\endgroup\right)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup + \left(\begingroup\color{teal}k_v\endgroup\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}v_0\endgroup\right)\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup\\
    &= \left(\vec{p} + \begingroup\color{red}u_0\endgroup\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}v_0\endgroup\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup\right) + 
    \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup \left(\begingroup\color{red}k_u\endgroup\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{a}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}k_v\endgroup\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{b}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \vec{q} + \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup(\vec{r})
\end{align*}

All of this, is just a single position vector of a point on the plane ($\vec{q}$), plus a vector ($\vec{r}$) living in the plane, scaled by $\color{brown}\lambda$ 

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/geodesic-plane.png}
\end{center}

Where the constants simply specify the initial position ($\color{red}u_0$ and $\color{teal}v_0$) and initial velocity ($\color{red}k_0$ and $\color{teal}k_0$).\\
So, given an initial position and velocity, there's a unique geodesic on the 2D surface, which is just the straight line heading in the direction 
of the velocity vector.\\

\subsubsection{Geodesics on a sphere}

Now we consider a 2D surface of a sphere, of radius 1.\\
Let's recall the XYZ equations defining the surface, condering the radius=1:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup &= \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
\end{align*}

We already calculated the first order derivative, i.e. the tangent basis vectors before, using the chain rule, as:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} 
    + \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
    - \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} &= -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    + \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
\end{align*}

And then the metric tensor:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{blue}g_{ij}\endgroup = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\\
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0\\
        0 & \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

Proceeding further, to compute the Christoffel symbols we'll need the second-order derivatives, so we can get:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\right) &=
    -\cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
    -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\\
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right) &=
    -\cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}\\
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\right) &=
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}\right) =
    -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    + \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
\end{align*}

We can also get easily the inverse metric tensor, as the metric tensor is diagonal, so its inverse is formed by the reciprocal of the components:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\mathfrak{g^{ij}}\endgroup = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0\\
        0 & 1/\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

So finally we're ready to compute the Christoffel symbols: it has 3 indexes, i, j and k, and each of those 
goes from 1 to 2, so that means there are 2 x 2 x 2 symbols to compute, for a total of 8.\\
Notice also that, in the formula there's also the l index on the right hand side, so there's a summation over l going on as well.\\

Let's start by considering the k=1 and k=2 equations, and explicitly write the summation over l:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{ij}\endgroup &= \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{11}\endgroup
    + \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{21}\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{ij}\endgroup &= \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{12}\endgroup
    + \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{22}\endgroup
\end{align*}

In this way, we clearly see the components of the inverse metric tensor in the equations, and we know that the off-diagonal components are equal to zero, hence, already plugging in the values:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{ij}\endgroup &= \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{11}\endgroup = 
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}\\
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{ij}\endgroup &=
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{22}\endgroup = 
    \frac{\partial^2 \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} \frac{1}{\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2}
\end{align*}

And now we need to compute all the dot products.\\
Normally, the dot product of two 3D vectors, we'd end up with 9 total terms, but since here we're dealing with the cartsian XYZ coordinate system, all the off-diagonal dot products go to zero, and the
diagonal terms are simply equal to 1, so at the end, in the cartesian system, the dot product is a simple sum of 3 multiplications:

\begin{align*}
    \text{ONLY IN CARTETIAN BASIS}\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{a}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{b}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{cyan}a^X\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}b^X\endgroup
    + \begingroup\color{cyan}a^Y\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}b^Y\endgroup
    + \begingroup\color{cyan}a^Z\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}b^Z\endgroup
\end{align*}

We start with the dot products:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \cdot
    \frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup^2} &= 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
    \end{bmatrix} \cdot
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -\cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
    \end{bmatrix} = -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup) + \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup) = 0
\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \cdot
    \frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup^2} &= 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
    \end{bmatrix} \cdot
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -\cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} = -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \cdot
    \frac{\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} &= 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
    \end{bmatrix} \cdot
    \begin{bmatrix}
        -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
        0
    \end{bmatrix} = 0
\\
\dots\\
\dots
\end{align*}

You can go ahead with all the calculations and you eventually find that the non-zero Christoffel symbols are:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{22}\endgroup &= -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{12}\endgroup &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{21}\endgroup = \frac{\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)}{\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)}
\end{align*}

Note that since the order of differentiation does not matter, you have that $\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij} = \color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ji}$\\

Now, plugging these into the geodesic equations, the only non-zero ones are:

\begin{align*}
    &\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{22}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 \\
    &\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{12}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{21}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + 2\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{12}\endgroup \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 \\
\end{align*}

We can plug in the Christoffel symbols, rewrite the coordinates back to $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$, and replace the $cos/sin = cot = 1/tan$:

\begin{align*}
    &\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup) \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 \\
    &\frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + 2 \cot(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = 0 \\
\end{align*}

Now there are non-linear couple of differential equations, not easy to solve in the general case.\\
To simplify the example, we can try some special cases like a circle of latitude on the sphere:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}\theta_0\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup = \begingroup\color{teal}k\endgroup\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup
\end{align*}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/sphere-lat.png}
\end{center}

In this way we have simple first derivative, and 0 second order derivatives:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} &= 0\\
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} &= \begingroup\color{teal}k\endgroup\\
    \\
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} &= 0\\
    \frac{d^2 \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} &= 0
\end{align*}

Bringing these into the geodesic equations, we obtain:

\begin{align*}
    &\cos(\begingroup\color{red}\theta_0\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}\theta_0\endgroup)\begingroup\color{teal}k\endgroup^2 = 0\\
    &0 = 0
\end{align*}

The second one is automatically true of course, and to make the first equation true, we need to choose the values of $\color{red}\theta_0$ and $\color{teal}k$ so that the term goes to 0.\\

You can set $\color{teal}k$ $=0$ but that's not very interesting because that's a single point at a constant $\color{teal}v$.
Another option is to make the $\sin$ part go to zero by setting $\sin(\begingroup\color{red}\theta_0\endgroup)$ $=0$, that's basically $\color{red}\theta_0$ $=0$ or $\color{red}\theta_0$ $=\pi$, but this also 
is not very interesting because it's a single point at north/south pole of the sphere.\\

The other option is to have the $\cos$ part go to zero, and this requires $\color{red}\theta_0$ $=\pi/2$ which is a curve around the equator.\\

So the path along the equator

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup &= \pi/2\\
    \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup &= \begingroup\color{teal}k\endgroup \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup
\end{align*}

is a geodesic curve on the sphere, which means that the shortest way to get between two points on the equator, is to travel straight along the equator
on the geodesic path.\\
Other circles of latitude are not geodesics, like if you take a latitude path on the northern emisphere, you will find out that the geodesic is not along
the same latitude path, but it curves slighlty above it.\\
The only circle of latitude that it's a geodesic, is the equator, which makes sense because if we think of traveling along the equator, our
accelerator vectors always point directly inward towards the center of the circle, which is also the center of the sphere, hence completely normal to the surface (geodesic requirement).
Instead, if we travel along another circle of latitude, the accelerator vectors point to the center of the circle, which in this case, is not the center of the sphere, so we end up with a tangential 
component as well as a normal component.\\
Now, the equator being a geodesic, by rotational symmetry, all great circles (which center is located at the center of the sphere) are geodesics.
