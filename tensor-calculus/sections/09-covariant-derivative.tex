\section{Covariant Derivative}

If we want to give a one-sentence definition of the  Covariant Derivative, we can
say it's the \textbf{understanding the rate of change of vector (tensor) fields
that takes changing basis vectors into account}

There's obviously some explanation and proof required, and we're going to define the 
Covariant Derivative first in the flat space, which is the easiest way to look at it, 
and then follow along, increasing the challenge, by looking at it in curved-space, first extrinsic and 
then intrinsic, and finally giving its most abstract definition.\\

\subsection{Covariant derivate in flat-space}

Let's start by recalling that, given a certain basis, we can define a vector $\color{orange}\vec{v}$ as a 
linear conbination of the basis vectors, for example in cartesian basis $\color{blue}\vec{e_i}$ or polar $\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_j}}$:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{cyan}v^i\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_i}\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{magenta}v^i\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_i}}\endgroup
\end{align*}

If we try to differentiate the vector with respect to one of the coordinates in cartesian basis in $\mathbb{R}^2$, let's say x:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} (\begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup) &= 
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^x\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^y\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup\right)\\ &=
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^x\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^y\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup\right)\\ &=
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^x\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^x\endgroup \cancel{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup\right)}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^y\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup + \cancel{\begingroup\color{cyan}v^y\endgroup \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup\right)}\\
    &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^x\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^y\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup
\end{align*}

where the canceled components are due to the fact that, in cartesian coordinates, the basis vectors are just constant, so the derivative is reduced to be 
a linear combination of the basis vectors, with coefficients given by the derivative of the vector components.\\

Different is the case of polar coordinates though, because the vector basis are not constant in space, but change from point to poing.
In polar coordinates, we end up with 4 components, 2 of them track the rate of change of components, and 2 of them track the change of the basis vectors.\\

In general, this is actually the simplest definition of the covariant derivative, so an ordinary derivative 
that takes into account the change of basis, so it makes sure to differentiate both the components and the basis vectors.\\
Writing that down using Einstein summation convention:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} (\begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup) &= 
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_j}\endgroup
    + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^j\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup}
\end{align*}

Sometimes, the basis vector derivative, is commonly expanded into a linear combination of the basis 
vectors themselves, so:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} &= 
    \begingroup\color{cyan}\Gamma^1_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}\Gamma^2_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\\
    &= \begingroup\color{cyan}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

So the covariant derivative can be re-written as:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} (\begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup) &= 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
    + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{cyan}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_k}\endgroup\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}v^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{cyan}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup\right)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

\subsection{Covariant derivate in curved-space - extrinsic}

So now we're moving on into looking at the rate of change of vector fields, on curved surfaces.\\
It turns out that it's a lot trickier dealing with vector spaces here, when compared to flat ones, and we're going to explain why.

Let's take these two vector fields here below, you can clearly visualize that the first one is constant, whereas the second one is not:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/constant-vs-non-constant-vf.png}
\end{center}

The first one is constant because we can see that all the vectors are the same...but how do we say that?
It may sounds silly and straight forward, but how do we know that these are the same? Well what we could do is take
a couple of these vectors shown in the first picture, and slide one on top of the other, and check if they overlap perfectly. That's quite
easy to do right?\\

But let's consider instead, a vector field existing on the surface of a 2D sphere, so every point on the sphere, has 
a vector associated to it. And now let's try to ask the same question: if we compare the blue vector and the red vector in the picture, 
how do we tell if they're the same?

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/vector-comp-sphere.png}
\end{center}

You might be tempted to do exactly the same thing we did before, taking the red vector, slide it over to the red
and find out that they overlap perfectly. There's a problem with this method though, and you can maybe realize it already.\\

If you imagine this sphere being a planet, and you stand still on the north pole where the red vector's origin is. To you, 
the red vector would look like pointing forward towards the horizon. If you imagine instead, standing still on the blue vector's origin point, 
the blue vector would look to you, as pointing towards the sky, perpendicular to the surface.\\
\textbf{So from the point of view of someone living on the 2D surface, these two vectors are clearly not the same.}

That means that if we're going to invent a derivative that makes sense for the "inhabitants" of the 2D surface, we need 
to come out with a new way of comparing vectors on the surface, that works for the observers living on the surface.\\

We're going to introduce an idea called \textbf{Parallel transport:} a way of moving 
vectors around on a surface that "keeps them straight".\\

How does this work? Imagine you standing on the sphere's north pole, holding an arrow towards the horizon in front of you.
Now imagine you want to transport this arrow down towards the equator of the sphere, while holding the arrow as straight as possible.\\
What this means is that while you walk along the surface towards the equator, the arrow will always point towards the horizon from your 
point of view, so it would look something like this:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/parallel-transport-01.png}
\end{center}

Of course, you can probably already see a problem with this parallel transport: if you imagine now, from the point 
of view of this person holding the arrow, traveling along the equator, always holding the arrow straight to him, and then 
walking backwards to the point it started on the north pole, you will find that the arrow/vector transported does not look like
when it started, so the starting and ending vectors are not the same:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/parallel-transport-02.png}
\end{center}

This means that \textbf{parallel transport DOES NOT keep vectors constant.}\\
The reason for that is that's \textbf{impossible to define a constant vector field on a curved surface.}\\
The parallel transport keeps vectors "as constant as possible" while moving along the surface step-by-step, and 
this is the best we can do, and the downside is that if traveling in a loop, we may and up
with a vector that looks differently than the one with started with.\\

Allright, now that we have the intuition, how do we describe this mathematically?
Referring to the last figure above, if we consider the first two vectors , the first at the north pole, and 
the next one moving towards the equator, and compare them, we can see that the change between them 
is this vector below, which points towards the center of the sphere, the same if we continue
comparing vectors in pair:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/parallel-transport-03.png}
\end{center}

So this means that, when we parallel transport a vector "forward", its rate of change is completely normal to the surface,
perpendicular to the tangent plane.\\
Bringing this vector along this path, basically gives us a vector field defined at every point along this curve, and 
this vector field obeys this relationship:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = \begingroup\color{red}\vec{n}\endgroup
\end{equation}
 
Or, in other words, the difference between the rate of change of this vector field, and its normal part, is equal ot $\vec{0}$:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} - \begingroup\color{red}\vec{n}\endgroup = \vec{0}
\end{equation}

So, the best that we can do to define a "constant" vector field on a curved surface, is parallel transporting a vector along
a curve, and this, is where the covariant derivative on curved surfaces come in.\\

\textbf{The covariant derivative on curved surfaces helps us find parallel transported vector fields.}\\

So we're going to define the covariant derivative on a curved surface, as the rate of change of a \textcolor{violet}{vector field} $\color{violet}\vec{v}$ in 
some \textcolor{teal}{direction} $\color{teal}\vec{w}$, with the \textcolor{red}{normal component, of the rate of change, subtracted.}.

\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{teal}\vec{w}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup
\end{equation}

So let's say we have a curve parametrized by the parameter $\color{brown}\lambda$, and as the parameter increases, we move forward along the curve. We have a vector field
$\color{violet}\vec{v}$ defined along this curve, and we want to find the rate of change of this vector field as we travel along the curve.\\

The covariant derivative of this vector field in a direction given by $d/d\lambda$, which is basically the direction 
along the curve, would be the rate of change of the vector field, in the lambda direction, with the component normal to the surface subtracted.

\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} - \begingroup\color{red}\vec{n}\endgroup
\end{equation}

And as we said, when this difference quantity is zero, it's when the vector field is being parallel transported along the curve.\\

So we can say that, when the covariant derivative $\nabla_{\begingroup\color{teal}\vec{w}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \vec{0}$, it means
that the vector field is parallel transported in the direction $\color{teal}\vec{w}$.\\

All of this can be clarified by giving an example, of the covariant derivative + parallel transport on a sphere of radius = 1.\\
We'll use the 2D plane of coordinates $\color{red}u^1$ and $\color{teal}u^2$, knowing from previous section, that the parametrization
of the sphere in 3D, using these 2D coordinates can be written as:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}u^2\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup &= \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}u^2\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)
\end{align*}

So, each point on the 2D plane $\color{red}u^1$, $\color{teal}u^2$ gives us a vector $\color{violet}\vec{R}$ which lives in the $\color{cyan}X, Y, Z$ 3D space.\\
The $\color{red}u^1$ curves are the lines of longitude running from north to south and the $\color{teal}u^2$ are the curves of latitude, 
running from west to east.\\
Also as a reminder, the tangent basis vectors on the sphere are given by the two partial derivatives of the position vector, where $\color{red}\vec{e_1}$ are  
vectors tangent to the $\color{red}u^1$ curves, and $\color{teal}\vec{e_2}$ are vectors tangent to the $\color{teal}u^2$ curves:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} &= \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}u^2\endgroup} &= \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{align*}

And now let's try to compute the covariant derivative of some \textcolor{violet}{tangent vector field} $\color{violet}\vec{v}$ in the direction of the $\color{red}u^i$ \textcolor{red}{coordinate}.\\
Please note that we're considering the \textbf{vector field to be tangent to the surface}, and in most physics application, this is indeed the case for curved surfaces.\\

Allright, so remind yourself that the covariant derivative, we just said it's the ordinary derivative with the normal component subtracted:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Being the vector field composed by vectors always tangent to the surface, we can expand them as a linear combination of the basis vectors:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \left[\begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right] - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \right] - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup
\end{align*}

As you can remember, we can write down the second term in the squared brackets, the derivative of the basis vectors, as a linear combination of the Chrystoffel symbols and Second Fundamental form:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \right] - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup\\
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup \right] + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup
\end{align*}

We can now factor out the $\color{red}\vec{e_k}$ changing the summation index of the first term, which is just a dummy one:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup 
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{teal}L_{ij}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup - \begingroup\color{red}\hat{n}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Since we need to subtract the whole normal component, we can just cancel out the last two terms, and what remains is just the tangential component:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup 
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{k}_{ij}\endgroup \right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

which is our final formula for the covariant derivative.\\

Before, in the geodesic part, we already derived how to compute the Chrystoffel symbols, so now we're ready to go proceed and actually calculate the covariant derivative in this case.
We also already calculated the symbols for the sphere of radius one, and the only non-zero ones are:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{1}_{22}\endgroup &= -\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{12}\endgroup &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{2}_{21}\endgroup = \frac{\cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)}{\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)}
\end{align*}

Our summations run over i, j and k for the covariant derivative, but considering only the non-zero terms, we're left with the following two 
formulas for $i=1$ ($\color{red}u^1$), and $i=2$ ($\color{teal}u^2$):

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup 
    &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup}\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup + \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup \cot(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\\
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup 
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} -  \frac{1}{2} \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup \sin(2 \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
    + \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup \cot(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{align*}

A practical example would be considering curve running from $\color{red}u^2 = 0$ to $\color{red}u^2 = \pi/2$, with constant $\color{red}u^1 = \pi/2$. Practically a curve where when the parameter increases, we run along the equator:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.15]{figures/covariant-sphere-01.png}
\end{center}

So the parametrization is:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup \rightarrow \left(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup = \pi/2, \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup = \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup\right)
\end{equation}

If we now consider a hyphotetical vector field where the first component is equal to $\color{red}\vec{e_1}$ when $\color{red}u^2 = 0$, 
and equal to $\color{red}\vec{e_2}$ when $\color{red}u^2 = \pi/2$:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup)\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
    + \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup)\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{equation}

So this is a vector field that's definitely changing while going along the equator curve above:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/covariant-sphere-02.png}
\end{center}

Intuitively then, we should expect its covariant derivative in the direction of the curve, to be different than zero, as this is clearly
not a parallel transport:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup 
    = \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup
    &= \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup\\
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} -  \frac{1}{2} \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup \sin(2 \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
    + \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup \cot(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup)\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Since $\color{red}u^1$ is constant and equal to $\pi/2$, the term with $\sin$ and $\cot$ go to zero, then:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
    + \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\\
    &= -\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup)\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup + \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup)\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Which is indeed confirming that the covariant derivative is non-zero, and \textbf{it makes completely sense if you consider the tangent planes 
for each of the vectors moving along the curve; They're sort of spinning around in the tangent planes, so its rate of change is not completely
normal to the surface, there's a tangential part that exists in the tangent plane}

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/covariant-sphere-03.png}
\end{center}

If you instead, consider another vector field, simply described by:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup
\end{equation}

along the same curve

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.2]{figures/covariant-sphere-04.png}
\end{center}

\textbf{And you try to compute its covariant derivative, you can easily see that all the terms go to zero. There's no vector 
change in the tangent planes, the whole rate of change happens along the normal direction with respect to the surface: we are 
parallel transporting this vector along the curve.}\\

SO what we've done so far was, starting from a given vector field, we computed the covariant derivative and from its result, 
we checked if the vector field involves the parallel transport of a vector.
What we'd like to do now is go in the opposite direction, so starting from a single vector, and given a curve, 
we will demand the parallel transport condition along a curve, and get the resulting vector field.\\

Let's start with the simple initial vector $\color{violet}\vec{v_0}$, on the equator curve described above, 
pointing along $\color{red}\vec{e_1}$ at the origin point of the curve. For this, we demand the parallel transport
condition of the covariant derivative along the curve to be zero:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup \rightarrow \left(\begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup = \pi/2, \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup = \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v_0}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup = \vec{0}
\end{equation}

We then start with the formula we used above, as before, the $\sin$ and $\cot$ terms go to zero, due to $\color{red}u^1$ being 
constant and equal to $\pi/2$:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup
    &= \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup
    + \left[\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup}\right]\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_2}\endgroup = \vec{0}
\end{align*}

What this requires, is that both the vector field components are equal to zero, which means they have to be some constants:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} &= 0 \\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup} &= 0
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup(\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup) &= c_1\\
    \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup(\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup) &= c_2
\end{align*}

Remember that we need to respect the initial condition right? At $\color{brown}\lambda$ $=0$ we had that $\color{magenta}v^1$ $= 1$ and 
$\color{magenta}v^2$ $= 0$, which simply means that the vector components have to always be constant and equal to:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{magenta}v^1\endgroup(\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup) &= c_1 = 1\\
    \begingroup\color{magenta}v^2\endgroup(\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup) &= c_2 = 0
\end{align*}

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup(\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup) = \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_1}\endgroup 
\end{equation}

This is the vector field resulting by requiring the parallel transport of the initial vector given.\\

\subsection{Covariant derivate in curved-space - intrinsic}

Allright and here we are with another definition of the covariant derivative, this time using intrinsic geometry.\\
You may wonder why we need this in the first place, if the extrinsic defintion gets the job done; Well the reason is that there are 
cases, like General Relativity, where we cannot use any extrinsic world embedding our 2D surface, simply because it does not exist such thing.
In GR, spacetime is a 4 dimentional space, and when Einstein explained his theory, it did not think about this space being inside a higher dimentional one, 
where we could leverage the use of an origin or position vectors.\\
The universe itself, the spacetime, is intrinsically curved with no outside space existing at all.\\

So, when working using intrinsic geometry, we can forget about a higher dimentional XYZ coordinates we can use, we can 
forget about position vectors, and global origin from where to define this.
Remember that intrinsic can also be seen as a "bug's" view of the surface, where basis vectors
are defined as partial derivative operators, and not derivative of the position vector like in extrinsic geometry.
They live in the Tangent Vector Space $T_pM$ at some point p.\\

So, let's try to compute the formula for the covariant derivative for intrinsically curved spaces. Before, we said that
the covariant derivative of a vector field, is the ordinary derivative, with the normal component of the rate of change subtracted, but since
we're dealing with intrinsic geometry now, there's no such thing as normal vectors, as this would imply there's an outside space 
we can point to, and this is not the case now.\\

So we ignore the normal part of the formula, and we can try writing down the covariant derivative, with the Christoffel symbols used to represent
the basis vectors derivative coefficients, and then just re-arranging the dummy index to factor out the basis vectors:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup &= 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\\
    &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}v^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} + \begingroup\color{magenta}v^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma_{ik}^k\endgroup\right)\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Now, the problem ends up being the Christoffel symbols, because before, when we derived the expression for these, we ended up with this formula, 
which requires the dot product of the basis vectors with the derivative of the basis vectors:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{m}_{ij}\endgroup = \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup } \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_l}\endgroup\right) \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{lm}\endgroup
\end{equation}

Since we don't have now, the XYZ extrinsic basis, we can't easily compute these dot products, so we need a new strategy to compute the Christoffel symbols.\\
There are two important facts to recognize before we proceed:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The metric tensor components are the dot products of the basis vectors $\begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup$
    \item The order of the dot product does not matter, hence the metric tensor components are symmetric in the $i$ and $j$ indexes $\color{red}g_{ij}$ $=$ $\color{red}g_{ji}$ 
    \item The Chrisoffel symbols are symmetric in their lower indexes, so $\color{blue}\Gamma_{ij}^k$ $=$ $\color{blue}\Gamma_{ji}^k$
\end{itemize}

Now, we start by taking the derivative of the metric tensor in the direction of the $\color{red}u_k$ coordinate, where we can leverage the metric tensor
definition, and an important property called \textbf{metric compatibility} (more on this in the next section, we take it for granted now), which allows us to use 
the derivative product rule applied to dot products:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup &= \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} \left(\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ik}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_l}\endgroup\right) \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cdot \left(\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_l}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ik}\endgroup \left(\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_l}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right) + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \left(\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_i}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ik}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{lj}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup
\end{align*}

We're free to swap the lower indexes of the metric tensor, so we'll do so for the first term and we obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}  = 
    \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ik}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{jl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup
\end{equation}

Now, note this down first, and then we're going to take other derivatives of the metric tensor, twice more, but with different indexes:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}g_{ki}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{kj}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}g_{jk}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ji}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ki}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{jl}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Let's watch carefully the three together, and I will color code the terms here:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial u^k} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ik}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}g_{jl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup\\
    \frac{\partial g_{ki}}{\partial u^j} &= \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{kj}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}\Gamma^{l}_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{teal}g_{kl}\endgroup\\
    \frac{\partial g_{jk}}{\partial u^i} &= \begingroup\color{teal}\Gamma^{l}_{ji}\endgroup \begingroup\color{teal}g_{kl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^{l}_{ki}\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}g_{jl}\endgroup
\end{align*}

The terms with the same color, are actually the same, so we can do something here, let's sum them in this way:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial u^k} + \frac{\partial g_{ki}}{\partial u^j} - \frac{\partial g_{jk}}{\partial u^i} &= 
    \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{kj}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup\\
     \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial u^k} + \frac{\partial g_{ki}}{\partial u^j} - \frac{\partial g_{jk}}{\partial u^i} &= 2 \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{li}\endgroup
\end{align*}

We can now sum on both sides, with the inverse metric tensor:

\begin{align*}
     \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{im}\endgroup \left(\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial u^k} + \frac{\partial g_{ki}}{\partial u^j} - \frac{\partial g_{jk}}{\partial u^i}\right) &= 2 \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{l}_{jk}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}g_{li}\endgroup \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{im}\endgroup
\end{align*}

Using the fact that the these metric tensors are inverse of each other, we finally obtain our new formula for the Christoffel symbols, only dependent on the metric tensor and its inverse:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^{m}_{jk}\endgroup = \frac{1}{2} \begingroup\color{purple}\mathfrak{g}^{im}\endgroup \left(\frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial u^k} + \frac{\partial g_{ki}}{\partial u^j} - \frac{\partial g_{jk}}{\partial u^i}\right)
\end{equation}

One last topic on this section, related to Geodesics. We previously saw that geodesics define the most straight path in a curve surface/space.
We can define this idea of walking forward along a geodesic, using the covariant derivative.\\
This idea of walking forward on a curved space/surface is captured, mathematically, by the idea of parallel transporting a vector along itself.
\textbf{When we say that a vector is parellel transported along itself, what we mean is that the vector we're taking the covariant derivative of, 
and the direction that we choose, are the same.}\\

\textbf{So when we say that the covariant derivative of a vector in the direction of itself is equal to zero, the resulting curve we get 
is a geodesic curve.}

If our curve is a function of the parameter $\color{brown}\lambda$, then the tangent vectors along the curve
would be given by the derivative operators $\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}$. A geodesic curve is described by the 
covariant derivative $\nabla_{\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} = \vec{0}$.\\

Let's try this out, first expanding using the multi-variable chain-rule, and then the derivative product rule:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} 
    &= \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    \vec{0} &= \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\right)\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup \partial\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)
\end{align*}

We can just re-write it now, using the basis vector notation and then use the Christoffel simbols to express 
the derivative of the basis vectors:

\begin{align*}
    \vec{0} &= \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup}{\partial\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2}\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_j}\endgroup + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

We can just replace the first term's dummy index with k and factor the $\color{red}\vec{e_k}$ basis vector out:

\begin{align*}
    \vec{0} &= \left(\frac{d^2\begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup^2} + 
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup\right) \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup
\end{align*}

This is, as you may remember, exactly the geodesic equation. It's just that now, with the covariant derivative notion, we can state that
the \textbf{geodesic is the curve resulting from paralle transporting a vector along itself.}

\subsection{Covariant derivative - abstract definition}

Now, finally, after having defined the covariant derivative first in flat space, then in curved spaces extrinsically, and afterward intrinsically, we're 
ready to give the most abstract, and at the same time, most powerful, definition of covariant derivative.

To save up some notation heavyness, we will use interchangeably, the following representations of the basis vectors:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_k}\endgroup \equiv \frac{\partial}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^k\endgroup} \equiv \begingroup\color{red}\partial_k\endgroup
\end{equation}

There are two main reasons why we want to study this abstract definition:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We're going to find that there are actually "several" covariant derivatives, not just one; The one we care about is called the \textcolor{red}{Levi-Civita connection}, which comes from the Fundamental Theorem of Rienmannian Geometry.
    \item It extends the covariant derivative to all tensor fields.
\end{itemize}

To help understanding how we arrived at the definition of the abstract covariant derivative, it helps to think about how another concept has been invented in the first place: the concept of 
vector spaces.\\

Let's start thinking about $\mathbb{R}^2$, the set of pairs of real numbers. The members of this set serve as an inspiration to the vector space idea.
If we start checking some properties of this set, you can clearly see that it's possible to find a reasonable way of adding two members of this set to obtain another
member of the set, also there's a reasonable way to scale the members by multiplying them by a scalar element, and obtain new members of the set.\\

Moreover, the order of addition does not matter, and we also find that there's a special zero element, that if we add to a member pair, gives back 
the same member pair. Each element also, had a negative version of it, that when added to the opposite member pair, gives the zero element.\\

On top of this, we have distribution properties for multiplication over addition:\\
For $\vec{u}, \vec{v}, \vec{w} \in \mathbb{R}^2$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\vec{v} + \vec{w} \in \mathbb{R}^2$
    \item $\vec{v} + \vec{w} = \vec{w} + \vec{v}$
    \item $\vec{u} + (\vec{v} + \vec{w}) = (\vec{u} + \vec{v}) + \vec{w}$
    \item $\exists \vec{0} \in \mathbb{R}^2 , \vec{v} + \vec{0} = \vec{v}$
    \item $\exists(-\vec{v}) \in \mathbb{R}^2 , \vec{v} + (-\vec{v}) = \vec{0}$
\end{itemize}

For $\vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^2, a, b \in \mathbb{R}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a\vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^2$
    \item $a(b\vec{v}) = (ab)\vec{v}$
    \item $1\vec{v} = \vec{v}$
\end{itemize}

For $\vec{v}, \vec{w} \in \mathbb{R}^2; a, b \in \mathbb{R}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a(\vec{v} + \vec{w}) a\vec{v} + a\vec{w}$
    \item $(a + b)\vec{v} = a\vec{v} + b\vec{v}$
\end{itemize}

We can take all these properties and turn them into a definition, we say that a vector space
is a set of vectors and a set of scalars, along with rules of addition and scaling, that follow the properties above.\\

So you see what the process was: we started with the properties of $\mathbb{R}^2$, and use them to define a vector space, 
which is a more abstract idea.
Now, given this new definition, we can find other examples of other things in mathematics, that obey the same rules, like the set
of N-tuples of real numbers, set of polynomials in one variable, the set of partial derivatives of coordinate variables, etc..\\

So, inventing the abstract definition of the covariant derivative, basically follows the same process. We start with the definition 
of the covariant derivative we're familiar with, look at some of its properties and then use those properties to define an abstract definition for it.
From there then, we can define many other types of covariant derivatives as well.\\

So we start by looking at some properties of the covariant derivatives:

\begin{itemize}
    \item $\nabla_{\begingroup\color{cyan}a\vec{w}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}b\vec{t}\endgroup}\vec{v} = \begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup \nabla_{\begingroup\color{cyan}\vec{w}\endgroup}\vec{v} + \begingroup\color{red}b\endgroup \nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\vec{t}\endgroup}\vec{v}$
    \item $\nabla_{\vec{w}}(\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup) = \nabla_{\vec{w}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup + \nabla_{\vec{w}}\begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup$
    \item $\nabla_{\vec{w}}(\begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup) = (\nabla_{\vec{w}} \begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup) \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup (\nabla_{\vec{w}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup)$
    \item $\nabla_{\begingroup\color{red}\partial_i\endgroup} (\begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup) = \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}a\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}$
\end{itemize}

So we're going to consider these 4 properties and take them as a definition of the abstract covariant derivative.
A covariant derivative is an operator with two input slot, it takes a direction vector, and an input field, and the output
will be the rate of change of that field along that direction, and this will be a new field.\\

Sometimes, this covariant derivative operator, is also called "Connection". This is because of the paralle transport condition. We saw
that in order to parallel transport a vector field along a curve, its covariant derivative has to be zero, if you look at this in terms of 
tangent vector spaces where we start and we end along the curve, the parallel transport basically provides a connection between tangent spaces in a curved space.\\

If you start at a point p, with the tangent space defined by $T_pM$, and end up at the point q with tangent space $T_qM$, the coveriant derivative provides you
a connection between these two tangent spaces in the curved space.\\

Now you remember how we defined the Christoffel symbols, well in this case we can simply write, in the most abstract way:

\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\vec{e_i}}\vec{e_j} = \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup\vec{e_k}
\end{equation}

Sometimes these are also called connection coefficients, and it turns out that, given the information we defined so far, 
we don't have enough information on how to solve for a unique set of these symbols, that's why we stated at the beginning of this section, 
that there are many possible covariant derivatives, it's because there are many possible ways to define these connection coefficients.\\

Though, there's a way we can get a unique solution for the Christoffel symbols, and that's by introducing two new properties called:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The torsion-free property;
    \item The metric compatibility property.
\end{itemize}

The torsion-free property says that, the covariant derivative of a basis vector $j$ along a direction $i$, is the same
as the covariant derivative of the basis vector $j$, in the $i$ direction:

\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\vec{e_i}}\vec{e_j} =  \nabla_{\vec{e_j}}\vec{e_i}
\end{equation}

This is what let us write, that the Christoffel symbols are symmetric on their lower indexed:

\begin{align*}
    \nabla_{\vec{e_i}}\vec{e_j} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup\vec{e_k}\\
    \nabla_{\vec{e_j}}\vec{e_i} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ji}\endgroup\vec{e_k}
\end{align*}

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ij}\endgroup = \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^k_{ji}\endgroup}
\end{equation}

The metric compatibility property is a sort of a special version of the derivation product rule, that's applied 
to the dot product:

\begin{equation}
    \nabla_{\vec{w}}(\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup) = (\nabla_{\vec{w}}\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup) \cdot \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup \cdot (\nabla_{\vec{w}}\begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup)
\end{equation}

This might be a weird rule, but in plain english, what this is saying is that \textbf{when we parallel transport two vectors, their dot product will stay the same.}\\

If you take these two vectors $\color{violet}\vec{v}$ and $\color{teal}\vec{u}$ and you parallel transport them, the condition is that their covariant derivative is $\vec{0}$, so if you plug
this result into the metric compatibility property, you find that the covariant derivative of their dot product is zero ($\nabla_{\vec{w}}(\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{v}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{u}\endgroup) = \vec{0}$), 
which simply means that the dot product stays constant. Which eventually means that the length of a parallel transported vector stays constant, and in case of considering two vectors, if parallel transported, their 
angle in between, stays constant.\\

One consequence of the metric compatibility property can be obtained if we replace the vectors with the basis vectors:

\begin{equation}
       \nabla_{\vec{e_k}}(\vec{e_i} \cdot \vec{e_j}) = (\nabla_{\vec{e_k}}\vec{e_i}) \cdot \vec{e_j} + \vec{e_i} \cdot (\nabla_{\vec{e_k}}\vec{e_j}) 
\end{equation}

We can replace the covariant derivatives with the Christoffel symbols:

\begin{equation}
       \nabla_{\vec{e_k}}(\vec{e_i} \cdot \vec{e_j}) = (\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ik}\endgroup\vec{e_l}) \cdot \vec{e_j} + \vec{e_i} \cdot (\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{jk}\endgroup\vec{e_l}) 
\end{equation}

The dot product is a scalar, so we can simply replace the covariant derivative with a partial derivative:

\begin{equation}
       \partial_k(\vec{e_i} \cdot \vec{e_j}) = (\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ik}\endgroup\vec{e_l}) \cdot \vec{e_j} + \vec{e_i} \cdot (\begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{jk}\endgroup\vec{e_l}) 
\end{equation}

Now, bringing the Christoffel symbols in front, will show the dot products, that we can simply re-write as metric tensor components:

\begin{align*}
    \partial_k(\vec{e_i} \cdot \vec{e_j}) &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ik}\endgroup(\vec{e_l} \cdot \vec{e_j}) + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{jk}\endgroup(\vec{e_i} \cdot \vec{e_l}) \\
    \partial_k(\begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup) &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ik}\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}g_{lj}\endgroup) + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{jk}\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup)
\end{align*}

Using the same, and switching around indexes, we can write down the following 3 equations then, and using the torsion-free property, we can color code the Christoffel symbols that
are the same, due to the symmetry of their lower indexes:

\begin{align*}
    \partial_k g_{ij} &= \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ik}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{jl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^l_{jk}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup\\
    \partial_j g_{ki} &= \begingroup\color{red}\Gamma^l_{kj}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{il}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{teal}\Gamma^l_{ij}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup\\
    \partial_i g_{jk} &= \begingroup\color{teal}\Gamma^l_{ji}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{kl}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{blue}\Gamma^l_{ki}\endgroup\begingroup\color{red}g_{jl}\endgroup
\end{align*}
