\section{The metric tensor and arch length - flat and curved spaces}

We already saw in the \textit{Tensor for beginners} notes, that the metric tensor is used to compute 
the length of a vector, the dot product between two vectors and the angle in between, and we also saw that 
it transforms using 2 covariant rules, hence is a (0,2)-tensor (bilinear form).

\begin{align*}
    g: V \times V \to \mathbb{R}\\
    g \left(\begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup, \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{w}\endgroup\right) \mapsto  \begingroup\color{orange}v^i\endgroup \begingroup\color{violet}w^j\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}g_{ij}\endgroup
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{g_{ij}}\endgroup &= F^k_i F^l_j \begingroup\color{blue}g_{kl}\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{blue}g_{kl}\endgroup &= B^i_k B^j_l \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{g_{ij}}\endgroup
\end{align*}

We'll here focus on using the metric tensor to compute lengths of curves in flat space.\\

\subsection{Arch length in flat spaces}
We recall that in order to get the length of a curve in multi-variable calculus, basically we approximate 
the curve by a series of straight line segments, sum them up and take the limit as the $\Delta$ parameter used
to parametrize the curve approaches to 0.\\
At the end of the day, the key to get the curve length is getting the magnitude of the vector $\frac{dR}{d\lambda}$ if $\lambda$ is the parameter, and $R$ the 
position vector used to describe a point on the curve. Which is nothing but the tangent velocity vector along the curve:

\begin{align*}
    \text{arc length} = \int \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup 
\end{align*}

Now, to get this magnitude, we can just compute the dot product of this vector with itself, as we know, and do that in cartesian coordinates for example:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right) \cdot
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}\right) +
    2 \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}\right) +
    \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2  \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}\right)
\end{align*}

This ugly long product chain will look similar in other coordinate systems, like polar:

\begin{align*}
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup}\right) +
    2 \frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup}\right) +
    \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2  \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup}\right)
\end{align*}

If you carefully look at this, you will find out that it's very similar to when we computed the magnitude of a vector in our
\textit{Tensor for beginners} notes:

\begin{align*}
    \|\begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup\|^2 &= \begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{orange}\vec{v}\endgroup\\
    &= \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^1\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^2\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\right)
    \cdot \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^1\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup + \begingroup\color{cyan}v^2\endgroup \begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\right)\\
    &= \bigl(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^1\endgroup\bigr)^2\left(\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup\right)
    + 2 \begingroup\color{cyan}v^1v^2\endgroup\left(\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_1}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\right)
    + \bigl(\begingroup\color{cyan}v^2\endgroup\bigr)^2\left(\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_2}\endgroup\right) \\ \\
    &= \bigl(\begingroup\color{magenta}\tilde{v^1}\endgroup\bigr)^2\left(\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_1}}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_1}}\endgroup\right)
    + 2 \begingroup\color{magenta}\tilde{v^1v^2}\endgroup\left(\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_1}}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_2}}\endgroup\right)
    + \bigl(\begingroup\color{magenta}\tilde{v^2}\endgroup\bigr)^2\left(\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_2}}\endgroup\cdot\begingroup\color{red}\tilde{\vec{e_2}}\endgroup\right)
\end{align*}

The key is indeed getting those dot products like we did in those initial notes, and those are nothing more 
than the components of the metric tensor in different coordinate systems.\\

\begin{align*}
    g_{\color{cyan}c^i} = \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup}\\
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}x\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}y\endgroup}
    \end{bmatrix} &&
    g_{\color{magenta}p^i} = \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup}\\
        \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}r\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup} & \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}\theta\endgroup}
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{align*}

Allright, now using the Einstein notation to save some writing:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}c^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^j\endgroup}\right) = \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}c^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}c^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}g_{ij}\endgroup\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}p^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}p^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^i\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^j\endgroup}\right) = \frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}p^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{magenta}p^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{g_{ij}}\endgroup
\end{align*}

And its transformation rules:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{g_{ij}}\endgroup = \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^k\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^i\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^l\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^j\endgroup} \begingroup\color{blue}g_{kl}\endgroup \\
    \begingroup\color{blue}g_{kl}\endgroup = \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^i\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^k\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{magenta}p^j\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}c^l\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}\tilde{g_{ij}}\endgroup
\end{align*}

\subsection{Arch length in curved spaces}
Now we'll move on computing arc lengths for curved space, and in particular we're going to focus on the surface of sphere for our maine example.\\

The first question we can ask ourselves is, how do we describe a 2D surface mathematically? Well, the most common and straight forward way is to start with a flat 2D 
plane, with $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$ coordinates, and then put this into some sort of function $\color{brown}f$ which will stretch and transform the plane so
that it becomes a 2D surface living in the 3D space basically:\\

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/surface-01.png}
\end{center}

The variables $\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$ become parameters of this vector function $\color{brown}f$ (i.e. its \textbf{domain} D contained in $\mathbb{R}^2$).
The range of the surfaces is contained in the three dimensional space $\mathbb{R}^3$, thus, a surface x is a mapping of D into $\mathbb{R}^3$.\\
This can be denoted by:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{brown}f\endgroup : D \to \mathbb{R}^3
\end{equation}

and also represented in 3D coordinate system:

\begin{align*}
    \left(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup\right) \rightarrow 
    \left(\begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup),
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup),
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\right)
\end{align*}

For a sphere surface, its parametrization/transformation function can be written as:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup &= \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
\end{align*}

Allright, and how do we represent a curve on a surface? Well we can apply the same logic, and make the curve, described by $\gamma$ with parameter $t$
on the $\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup$-plane go through the same transformation function $\color{brown}f$, to get the curve
in the 3D space:

\begin{center}
\includegraphics[scale=0.3]{figures/surface-02.png}
\end{center}

A more mathematically correct definition might be, that given a parametrization for a surface and a curve $\gamma$, 
the curve is on the surface, if a parametrization of $\gamma$ with a parameter $t$ can be obtained from equations of the 
surface when $u$ and $v$ become functions of $t$:

\begin{equation}
    \gamma(t) = \begingroup\color{brown}f\endgroup(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup(t),\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup(t))
\end{equation}

Let's see a simple example of a curve given by:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup \rightarrow (\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup = \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup = \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)
\end{equation}

which is just a simple diagonal line: 

\begin{center}
\input{figures/02-simple-curve.tex}
\end{center}

Its parametrization on the spherical surface can be obtained by applying the transformation functions we saw:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup &= \sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)
\end{align*}

If we now want to compute the length of such curve, we need to compute the magnitude of the tangent vector $\frac{dR}{d\lambda}$ where $R$ is just
the position vector of a point on the curve, in the 3D space X,Y,Z.\\

If we try to do that, we obtain:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right) \cdot
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} + 
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\right)\\
    &+ \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\right)
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\right)\\
    &+ \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}\right)
    + \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}\right)
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\right)
\end{align*}

Due to the orthonormality of the cartesian 3D basis vectors, you would end up with the simple Pythagora's theorem in 3D in this specific case:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2
\end{align*}

So we can now proceed computing these derivatives of $X, Y, Z$ wrt $\lambda$ and get:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} &= 
    \cos(2\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\\
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} &= 
    \sin(2\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)\\
    \frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup} &= 
    -\sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2 
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2
    + \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{brown}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2\\
    &= \left(\cos(2\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2\right)
    + \left(\sin(2\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2\right)
    + \left(-\sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2\right)\\
    &= 1 + \sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2
\end{align*}

So finally, our arc length is:

\begin{align*}
    \text{arc length} &= \int_{0}^{1} \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}\\
    &= \int_{0}^{1} \sqrt{1 + \sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2} d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup \approx 1.12389
\end{align*}

It's interesting to notice that this is not equal to the length of the same curve in the flat uv-plane, which would just be $\sqrt{2}$.\\
That big ugly formula we used in the first place, can be rewritten as a series of row array (1 x 3) - matrix (3 x 3) - column array (3 x 1) multiplications.

The 3 x 3 matrix composed by the dot products of the basis vectors, is nothing but the metric tensor in the 3D cartesian coordinate system, which we know 
actually being indeed the 3 x 3 Identity matrix at the end of the day, due to the orthonormality of the basis.\\

Something we need to realize, is that to compute this arc length in the curved surface (sphere in this case) we used the $XYZ$ coordinate system, so even though
we were analyzing the 2D surface, we then worked in the 3D space around the surface, in order to understand it.
This approach of studying surfaces is called \textbf{extrinsic} geometry, because it involves analyzing curved 2D spaces, using the sorrounding
3D space that the surface lives in.\\
The other way of analyzing surfaces is without looking at the sorrounding space, where we would only be looking at the uv-plane, and this point of view is called
\textbf{intrinsic} geometry.\\

Using the instrinsic approach, instead of studying the tangent vectors in the $XYZ$ space, we would be studying it in terms of the 
$\color{red}u$ and $\color{teal}v$ 2D coordinates:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:velocity-vector}
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} 
    = \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{d\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)
\end{equation}

Calculating the magnitude using this tangent vector would give us, in a more compact Einstein notation, considering $\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup = \begingroup\color{red}u^1\endgroup, \begingroup\color{red}u^2\endgroup$:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right) \cdot 
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup
\end{align*}

where $\color{red}g_{ij}$ is the metric tensor of the 2D uv-plane, i.e. the dot products of the basis vectors.\\

Allright, and how do we get the tangent space basis for the uv-plane? So we can compute the metric tensor...well you remember that:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup &= \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup)\sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\\
    \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)
\end{align*}

We can expand the tangent basis vectors using the chain rule:

\begin{align*}
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} &=
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}\\
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} &=
    \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}X\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Y\endgroup}
    + \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup} \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{cyan}Z\endgroup}
\end{align*}

Calculating those partial derivatives we get the tangent basis vectors $\begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup$ in the uv-plane, as linear combination of the 3D basis vectors $X, Y ,Z$:

\begin{align*}
    \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup &= \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup 
    + \sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \cos(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup
    - \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_z}\endgroup\\
    \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup &= -\sin(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_x}\endgroup 
    + \cos(\begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup) \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)\begingroup\color{blue}\vec{e_y}\endgroup
\end{align*}

And now we're able to compute the metric tensor using the basis vector dot products, and 
performing the calculations you will end up with:

\begin{equation}
    \begin{bmatrix}
        \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup & \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup\\
        \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{red}\vec{e_u}\endgroup & \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup \cdot \begingroup\color{teal}\vec{e_v}\endgroup
    \end{bmatrix} = 
    \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0\\
        0 & \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation}

And finally, we can compute the arc length as per the equation above:

\begin{align*}
    \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}^2 &=
    \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \cdot \frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
    &= \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right) \cdot 
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    \left(\frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup} \cdot \frac{\partial \begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{\partial \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}\right)\\
    &= \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^i\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u^j\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} \begingroup\color{red}g_{ij}\endgroup\\
    &= \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} & \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & 0\\
        0 & \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2
    \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\\
        \frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}
    \end{bmatrix} \\
    &= \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2
    + \sin(\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup)^2 \left(\frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}\right)^2
\end{align*}

If the arc length we're interested about is given, as we said before, by simple:

\begin{equation}
    \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup \rightarrow (\begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup = \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup, \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup = \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)
\end{equation}

Then we have that $\frac{d \begingroup\color{red}u\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} = 1$ and $\frac{d \begingroup\color{teal}v\endgroup}{d \begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup} = 1$, so
we end up with exactly the same result we got when using the extrinsic approach:

\begin{align*}
    \text{arc length} &= \int_{0}^{1} \norm{\frac{d\begingroup\color{violet}\vec{R}\endgroup}{d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup}}\\
    &= \int_{0}^{1} \sqrt{1 + \sin(\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup)^2} d\begingroup\color{orange}\lambda\endgroup \approx 1.12389
\end{align*}

\textbf{Main take away from this is that, in curved space, you can't trust your eyeballs, you can only trust the metric tensor}

You may wonder one thing, based on the above it seems that in order to get the intrinsic metric tensor matrix, you need to reference
the "outside space" anyway. That's actually somewhat true, but there are options to get the intrinsic one from several places:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Using indeed the extrinsic basis
    \item Invent one out of imagination
    \item Obtain it from another equation, like the Einstein Field Equations
\end{itemize}

Once you have the intrinsic matric, we then can do ALL calculations involving distances and angles, completely intrinsically.
But you need that first, and sometimes this involves getting help from the outside space at the start.\\

The last thing that may still bother you, is the fact that, based on our previous discussions, even in intrinsic geometry we 
refer to the position vector $\color{violet}\vec{R}$, which references the space outside our curved surface.\\
If we want to stay completely intrinsic in the surface, we can just remove the position vector from the equations completely, and 
treat the derivative operators themselves as the vectors
